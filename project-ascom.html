Ascom is a Gothenburg-based Ericsson spin-off serving healthcare and enterprise with mission-critical communication solutions, including medical device handsets. By the early 2010s they were feeling competitive pressure from smartphone-based alternatives and started developing a new generation of Android-based handsets — running on their own rugged hardware with a custom launcher and purpose-built apps.

As UX lead I worked with local and offshored development teams to build a product that lived up to the very high requirements of the medical industry, while genuinely innovating on the user experience for nurses. The Myco handset featured an integrated top display, charging station, barcode scanner, and a sturdy clip designed for the hospital environment.

Myco — Patient alert view

The core challenge was designing for a context where the stakes are genuinely high. Nurses on a ward are moving fast, often wearing gloves, responding to alerts under time pressure. The UI had to be immediately legible — large touch targets, clear alert hierarchy, no ambiguity.

The patient room view distilled the nurse's world into a single glanceable screen: which patients are assigned to them, and which need attention right now. Red for active alert, neutral for all clear. No unnecessary information, no chrome.

The hardware had its own UX requirements: the top display showed caller ID and alert status without unlocking the device; the clip position and grip angle were tested with nurses in the field; the charging station interface gave ward managers visibility across all devices at a glance.

The challenge

Designing for a world that wasn't built for design

Ascom's existing product line was built on decades of hardware engineering expertise. The shift to Android required rethinking the entire interaction model — but within a procurement and certification environment built for medical devices, not consumer software.

That meant navigating hardware supplier relationships, manufacturing tolerances, and regulatory constraints alongside the usual product design work. I worked directly with hardware suppliers on grip ergonomics, display positioning, and clip mechanics — physical prototyping alongside digital.

"Eventually I even jumped in as an actor for some of the graphic materials and videos for the launch."

I was embedded with the mobility team through to product launch — which meant being involved well beyond what a typical UX engagement covers. Research, prototyping, hardware iteration, launch materials. The product was considered a successful launch and has since been followed up with several more generations of the Myco.

How I worked

01

On-site research in hospitals

Over two years I conducted user research through on-site interviews, user tests, and contextual shadowing at hospitals in Sweden and Denmark. Watching nurses work — the actual environment, the pace, the gloves, the interruptions — shaped every decision. Abstract usability principles matter a lot less than watching someone try to read an alert while rushing down a corridor.

02

Iterative design across software and hardware

I designed and prototyped solutions for both software and hardware in parallel iterations. This wasn't common in UX work at the time — crossing into hardware meant working with suppliers and manufacturing partners to get physical details right. Grip angle, button placement, clip attachment, top display reading distance — all tested and revised across multiple prototyping cycles.

03

Working across local and offshored teams

The development team was distributed — local Gothenburg engineers alongside offshored development partners. As UX lead I was the bridge: ensuring design decisions translated cleanly across time zones and cultural contexts, and that the rationale behind UX choices was documented well enough to survive the distance.

What I took from this

01
Real context changes everything

No amount of persona work or scenario planning replaces watching a nurse manage a cardiac alert on a busy ward. The on-site research wasn't a box-tick — it was the design. The product decisions that held up best were the ones that came directly from what we saw in the field.

02
UX doesn't stop at the screen

Working across software and hardware was unusual at the time but it forced a more complete view of the product. The grip, the weight, the clip angle, the top display — these were UX problems as much as engineering ones. Expanding the definition of what design covers made the product better.

03
Longevity as a quality signal

The fact that Ascom continued building on the Myco platform for multiple product generations is a strong signal. Design decisions made early in a platform's life have compounding effects — getting the foundations right matters more than the surface-level executions that follow.
