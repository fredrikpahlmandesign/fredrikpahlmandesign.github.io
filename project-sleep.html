Sleep Cycle is a sleep-tracking app used by millions worldwide. I joined as Senior Product Designer in the Activation team, working on converting free users to paid subscribers through better onboarding, paywalls, and feature design.

Alongside the design work, I took on the role of UX & Discovery Lead — responsible for building a continuous discovery practice across four product teams from scratch. These two tracks fed each other in ways that changed how I think about both.

01

Discovery Practice

Building continuous discovery
from scratch

When I joined, there was no structured discovery practice. User research happened sporadically, insights rarely made it into decisions, and each team was operating largely on assumptions.

I set up a weekly rhythm: every week, across all four teams, we ran user interviews. Not as a project, not tied to a launch — just a standing cadence. We've now maintained that without interruption for 18 months.

The harder part was what to do with everything we were learning. I built a JTBD-based insight system in Notion that made research findable and connectable — so designers and PMs could pull from a growing body of knowledge rather than starting from scratch each time.

Over time I brought AI into the synthesis process: using Claude to help identify patterns across interview transcripts, spot contradictions between weeks, and surface connections to existing insights. The goal was keeping the cadence sustainable without drowning in raw data.

The cadence principle

Continuous discovery only works if it's not treated as a project. The weekly interviews had to become boring infrastructure — predictable, low-stakes, always on. When that clicks, the quality of decisions changes.

AI as synthesis partner

The bottleneck in discovery isn't gathering insight — it's making sense of it fast enough to be useful. AI-assisted synthesis didn't replace judgment, it meant we could keep up with the volume we were generating.

02

Design Craft

Activation, paywalls,
and feature design

As a designer in the Activation team, the brief was conversion — moving users from free to paid in a way that felt earned rather than coerced. That meant rethinking onboarding, paywalls, and the moments where the product's value becomes clear enough to justify paying.

A lot of the work was about sequencing: what do users need to experience before a paywall makes sense? What's the right gate to put on which features? When does a soft paywall actually convert better than a hard one?

Discovery fed directly into these questions. Running weekly interviews meant we were constantly updating our picture of where users were in their journey, what they found confusing, and what they valued enough to pay for.

03

Workflow Experiment

Using AI to generate
design assets and motion

Working in a small design team at a product company meant the pace of execution mattered. I started experimenting with AI-assisted generation not as a replacement for design judgment, but as a way to collapse the gap between concept and testable asset.

For campaign and marketing work — onboarding animations, contextual ad creatives, paywall visuals — I explored using AI to generate imagery and motion in a way that matched the Sleep Cycle visual language without requiring a production cycle for each variant.

The experiments shifted how I think about the designer's role: less about making every pixel, more about specifying the right language precisely enough that generation produces something worth shipping, or something close enough to iterate quickly from.
