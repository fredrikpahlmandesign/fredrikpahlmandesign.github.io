Turbu+ is an asthma and COPD service — a patient-facing mobile app paired with a connected inhaler and a Health Care Professional web portal for clinical follow-up. Designed, tested, and built from concept to launch on three markets.

A global pharma company exploring behaviour design as a way to strengthen their value proposition is impressive and it is a daunting task. A lot of my energy went towards work-shopping a large and diverse stakeholder group from across the organisation, maintaining the goal: to deliver a service that creates better patient outcomes than the drugs do on their own.

The product

Approach

"The idea that design can be something that ties in with business and project goals seemed new to many in the project."
01

Stakeholder alignment through Impact Mapping

The stakeholder group was large, globally distributed, and diverse. I challenged the client to connect design decisions closely to project and business goals — so the team wouldn't get stuck in committee design and fights over personal taste. We worked with an Impact Map to track the connections between business goals, customer needs and behaviour, and design solutions. Running it in Miro made it possible to keep everyone on the same page across geographies.

02

Research and user testing across three markets

I ran workshops, planned and performed research and user testing with the team. We worked mostly in Sweden but conducted workshops and research in the UK and Germany. As lead I was our main point of contact with the client and with our development partner — translating between business requirements, clinical constraints, and product decisions.

03

Design system for a regulated environment

It was tough to keep up production speed in an environment optimised for pharmaceutical molecules rather than pixels. Heavy traditional design specs — wireframes and flowcharts — were required as part of the legal process for developing a medical device. We had to get a lot of output from a limited team while keeping developers unblocked.

Sketch + Component Libraries Abstract (version control) inVision (handoff) Miro (impact mapping)
04

Team autonomy under constraints

I had to keep the team involved enough in discussions so they could operate and make decisions autonomously, while still providing them uninterrupted time to do the value-adding work. Led the design concept and made sure it adapted as the complexity of the project unfolded — over three years and ten designers, maintaining coherence is a real challenge.

What I took from this

01
Design leadership is mostly stakeholder work

A project like this is won or lost in the room with the client, not in Figma. The biggest lever was getting PMs, market area managers, and software architects to discuss detailed design decisions in the context of patient needs — rather than personal preference. Once that shift happened, design reviews changed entirely.

02
Constraints can be a design tool

The medical device regulatory process forced us to document in ways most digital product teams never would. It was slow, but it also meant every decision had a rationale. The discipline it imposed on the team's thinking wasn't entirely bad — it just needed to be redirected toward user needs rather than compliance paperwork.

03
Better patient outcomes are a different kind of KPI

When the early field results came in showing improved outcomes for patients using the system, it reframed everything. Retention rates and engagement metrics are fine. Knowing the thing you designed is helping people manage a chronic lung condition better is something else. It made the difficult parts worth it.
