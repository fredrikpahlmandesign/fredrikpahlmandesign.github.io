CarbonCloud helps food companies calculate, verify, and communicate the climate footprint of their products. I joined as the first design hire when the company was ten people and left four years later as Head of Product, having grown with the company through its most intense scaling phase.

Two things made this role unusual. First, the product span: a web platform for complex climate science used by food industry customers, and physical climate labels that ended up on supermarket shelves. Second, the career arc: I didn't arrive as Head of Product — the company needed more, and I grew into it.

01

Designing the product
Web platform, climate labels, and the feedback loop in between

The platform

CarbonCloud's core product is a web platform that lets food companies calculate the climate footprint of every ingredient, process, and distribution step in their supply chain. It's complex data work dressed up in an interface that needs to feel accessible to procurement managers and sustainability leads — not just climate scientists.

Building it from scratch meant making decisions about information architecture, data visualisation, and workflow design while the underlying science was still being refined. The product and the research it was built on co-evolved. Customer feedback wasn't a phase — it was ongoing, direct, and often came with deep domain expertise from the people using it.

Climate labels

The labels ended up on supermarket shelves. That's a different kind of design accountability.

Alongside the platform, CarbonCloud developed a climate label — a physical artefact that communicates a product's footprint at the point of sale. Designing for print, for packaging constraints, for consumer legibility, and for the regulatory requirements of multiple markets is a genuinely different challenge from screen design.

The label design went through multiple iterations driven by customer testing, retail feedback, and evolving best-practice frameworks for environmental claims. It had to work at small sizes on packaging, be understood by shoppers in seconds, and hold up to scrutiny from sustainability journalists and regulators alike.

02

Growing into the role
From first designer to Head of Product

"I didn't arrive with the title. The company needed more, and I grew into it — one stage at a time."

2020 – 2021

↓

UX Designer — First design hire

Joined a 10-person company with no existing design practice. Built the first design system, established the product's visual language, and set up workflows for working with the development team. The product was early-stage; customer feedback was direct and rapid, and the design decisions of those first months shaped everything that followed.

2021 – 2022

↓

VP of UX — Building the design function

As the company grew and commercial traction built, design needed to scale with it. I moved into a VP role responsible for the design function — hiring, establishing quality standards, and starting to bridge design and product strategy. This was the period where the gap between design output and product direction started to matter most.

2022 – 2024

Head of Product — Owning strategy and roadmap

Took full ownership of product strategy, the roadmap, and a product team of three. The shift that mattered most: moving the organisation from feature delivery to outcome-oriented development. That meant changing how we wrote roadmap items, how we worked with commercial and GTM leadership, and how we defined success. It was as much an organisational change as a product one.

What I took from this

01
B2B feedback is a design superpower

CarbonCloud's customers were sustainability managers, procurement leads, and food scientists — people with serious domain expertise who were using the product daily for high-stakes decisions. That feedback loop was faster and more substantive than most consumer products I'd worked on. It made the design work sharper and kept the product grounded.

02
Physical and digital require different honesty

A bad screen interaction can be updated tomorrow. A climate label printed on a million food packages cannot. The accountability that comes with physical design — for consumer understanding, for regulatory compliance, for the credibility of the company's core claim — was a useful discipline to bring back to the digital side of the product.

03
Titles follow clarity of contribution

The move from designer to Head of Product wasn't linear and it wasn't purely about ambition. The company needed someone to own the product direction, and I was already doing much of that work. The lesson: if you're clear about what a business needs and can do the work, the role tends to follow. The title is the last thing to update.
